Two-component model: retrievability R = e−t/S is the probability you can recall the item now; stability S is how slowly that probability decays. A successful review resets R to 1 and multiplies S — here by 1 + 4·(1−R), a toy version of the stability gain in modern schedulers (FSRS-family): the closer you are to forgetting, the more the review strengthens the memory.

Every fact you learn starts dying immediately. Not linearly — exponentially, like a radioactive isotope. The curve above is the modern formalization: your chance of recalling something t days after you last touched it is roughly R = e−t/S, where S — the memory's stability — is its half-life's cousin: the number of days for recall odds to fall to 1/e ≈ 37%.

The whole trick of spaced repetition lives in one asymmetry you can feel in the chart: decay is free, but so is the reset. One review, placed where the curve has sagged but not collapsed, buys you a new curve that falls several times slower. Do it again and the curve flattens again. Forgetting never stops — you just keep making it slower than your review schedule.

This essay walks the idea from its 1885 birth in a Berlin study chamber, through the algorithm running inside Anki and Obsidian's spaced-repetition plugins, to the one dial that actually controls your daily review workload.

§ 01Berlin, 1885 · the first measurement

Ebbinghaus counts his own forgetting

Hermann Ebbinghaus had a problem: you can't measure memory for meaningful material, because meaning contaminates the measurement — you remember King Lear partly because you understand it. So he built meaningless material: roughly 2,300 nonsense syllables — consonant–vowel–consonant triplets like DAX, BOK, YAT — and spent years drilling random lists of them into his own head, alone, to the beat of a metronome.

His instrument was the savings method. Learn a list until you can recite it twice perfectly, and note the time. Wait. Relearn the same list to the same criterion, and note the time again. Whatever you saved is what your memory kept:

savings = (Toriginal − Trelearn) / Toriginal

It's a beautifully sensitive measure — it detects memory you can no longer consciously recall. The seven points below are his published averages, and they fall off a cliff: after 20 minutes he had already lost about 42% of the work; after a day, two-thirds; after a month, four-fifths — savings of just 21.1% at 31 days.

Points: Ebbinghaus's seven published savings scores (58.2% at ~20 min → 21.1% at 31 days), replicated in 2015 by Murre & Dros. Solid curve: his own fitted law, b = 100k / ((log t)c + k) with k = 1.84, c = 1.25, t in minutes. Dotted: a single exponential e−t/S anchored to the 1-day point — it badly misses both ends, which is the tell that memory is not one decay process but a mixture of traces with different stabilities.

Two things in this chart matter for everything that follows. First, the curve is steep then merciful: most forgetting happens absurdly early, but what survives the first days decays slowly. Second, no single exponential fits it — the data spans five orders of magnitude in time, and only a mixture of fast-dying and slow-dying traces (or equivalently, a power law) can bend that way. Each review you do converts fast-dying memory into slower-dying memory. That conversion is the entire business model of spaced repetition.

§ 02The spacing effect · same effort, different memory

Five reviews, two destinies

Here is the most replicated result in learning science, over a century old and still routinely ignored by students the night before an exam: identical study time produces wildly different retention depending on when it happens. Cramming five reviews into five consecutive days feels productive — retrievability stays pinned near 100% all week. But each review arrives while the memory is still fresh, so it strengthens almost nothing. Space the same five reviews out, letting the curve sag before each one, and every review does real work.

Both learners use the same memory model as the hero (S₀ = 2 d, gain = 1 + 4·(1−R), every review succeeds). Massed: days 0–4. Spaced: gaps grow geometrically by the factor you set. The spacing effect is built into the gain term — reviews at low R strengthen more — which is the model's summary of the empirical literature, not an extra assumption on top of it.

The shaded areas are the honest score: average probability of recall across the whole 60 days. The crammer wins the first week and forfeits the other seven. And notice the endgame numbers — the spaced learner's final review leaves the memory with a stability of months, so the curve barely droops afterward. The crammer's five reviews, for the same total effort, built a memory that still dies in about two weeks.

So the ideal schedule is obvious in principle: wait as long as you dare before each review. The question a real system must answer is how long is "as long as you dare" for this card, for this person, today? In 1987, a Polish college student named Piotr Woźniak answered it with about ten lines of arithmetic.

§ 03SuperMemo-2, 1987 · the algorithm in your app

SM-2: ten lines that schedule a million cards

Woźniak's SM-2 is still, nearly forty years on, the beating heart of Anki and of Obsidian's spaced-repetition plugin. Each card carries an ease factor (EF, starting at 2.5) — a per-card estimate of how forgiving it is. After every review you grade yourself 0–5, and three rules fire:

Intervals grow geometrically. First success: review again in 1 day. Second: 6 days. After that, each new interval is the previous one times EF — so a card at EF 2.5 marches 1 → 6 → 15 → 38 → 94 days.

Ease drifts with your grades. After each review the ease updates:

EF′ = EF + (0.1 − (5−q)·(0.08 + (5−q)·0.02)),  EF′ ≥ 1.3

A grade of 5 nudges ease up by 0.10; a 4 leaves it alone; a 3 costs 0.14. The 1.3 floor is where "ease hell" lives — cards that keep getting graded 3 pile up at the minimum and return relentlessly.

Failure resets the ladder. Any grade below 3 sends the card back to a 1-day interval and restarts the 1 → 6 → ×EF progression (the ease factor itself is left unchanged on a failed grade, per the original algorithm).

The virtual learner is probabilistic: each card has a hidden difficulty; its true memory follows the stability model, and recall at each review succeeds with probability R — grades are then derived from how hard the recall felt. SM-2 sees only the grades, exactly like a real app. Two new cards are introduced per day for the first 15 days.

Grades follow the original SM-2 scale: 0–2 are failures (interval resets to 1 day, repetition count restarts), 3–5 are passes. Hollow marks on the strip are each card's next scheduled review. Time between cards is simulated — grading a card jumps the clock to whenever the next card comes due.

SM-2's genius is economy: two numbers per card (ease and interval) and one honest confession — the algorithm doesn't model your memory at all. It models your grades. Modern successors like FSRS go further and fit the actual stability–retrievability curve from the hero section to your review history, per card, which is why they can do something SM-2 can't: let you pick a target retention and schedule backwards from it. Which raises the question that section 04 exists to answer.

§ 04The economics · retention is a dial with a price

The workload cliff

Every scheduler is implicitly choosing a point on one curve: how much do you want to remember, and how many reviews per day are you willing to pay for it? Higher target retention means reviewing every card while its recall odds are still high — shorter intervals, and (because reviews at high R strengthen memories less) slower interval growth on top. The cost compounds. Push the target from 90% toward 97% and watch the daily load climb; the last few percent are the expensive ones.

Two practical readings of this chart. If your reviews feel endless, the cheapest fix is usually not "study harder" — it's lowering target retention a few points, or admitting the new-cards dial is set to a fantasy. And the reverse: retention below ~85% is a false economy, because the failure rate itself starts generating relearning reviews. For most people, most material, the flat part of the valley sits near 85–92% — which is why 90% has been the default in this field for thirty years.

§ 05What to actually do with this

Takeaways

Forgetting is the default, not a failure.

Ebbinghaus lost two-thirds of his perfectly-memorized lists in a day, and he was the most motivated learner in the history of the field. Any knowledge system that doesn't schedule re-exposure is a system for manufacturing the feeling of having learned.

Review at the sag, not at the peak.

A review while recall is still easy is nearly wasted; a review just before the memory dies is maximally strengthening. Desirable difficulty is the mechanism, not a slogan — you saw it as the (1−R) term in every model above.

The schedule matters more than the software.

SM-2 from 1987 captures most of the value: expanding intervals, per-card ease, reset on failure. FSRS refines the same two-component model. Switching apps buys you little; actually clearing your due queue buys you everything.

Choose retention like a budget, because it is one.

Target retention is the one dial that sets your daily tax. 90% is the thirty-year default for a reason; 97% roughly doubles the bill for a difference you'll rarely notice. Spend the saved reviews on more material instead.

Fewer, better cards beat heroic schedules.

Every model here assumed the card is atomic and well-formed. In practice, card quality moves your true stability more than any algorithm: one fact per card, cues that match how you'll need the memory, and delete without guilt. The curve forgives good cards.
